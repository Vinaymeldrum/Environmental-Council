Impacts of Climate Change

Climate change is a significant change in long-term weather patterns1. This could be a change in the average precipitation of a region, or a change in the average temperature2. Global warming is a measure of climate change and represents an increase in average global temperature3. There are many impacts of climate change, ranging from rising sea levels and melting glaciers to floods, droughts and heat waves.

The average temperature of the planet's surface has increased by about 1.1°C since the end of the 19th century due to human emissions into the atmosphere4. Greenland lost around 150 to 250 km3 of ice between 2002 and 2006, and Antarctica lost about 152 km3 of ice between 2002 and 20055. Average temperatures in the Arctic have increased by about 5°C over the past 100 years6. Recent data shows that there will be almost no ice remaining in the Arctic in the coming decades7. The sea level has increased about 8 inches over the last century8. Several large cities in the world would be flooded because of rising sea levels, so many people would be displaced9.

Climate change can cause extreme weather conditions such as floods, tornadoes, lightning, hurricanes, droughts, heat waves, heavy rainfall, and snowstorms. In the future, if we do not slow down climate change, hurricanes will become stronger and more intense and sea levels will increase by 1 to 4 feet by 2100, which will increase flooding in many areas10. Higher temperatures lead to droughts. Decreasing rainfall can cause droughts or turn a region into a desert11. When the soil becomes dry, the sun's heat will warm the soil and air as there is no moisture to evaporate, resulting in warmer and drier conditions12. This can cause famines because agriculture is impossible. Droughts or desertification can affect drinking water levels in underground reservoirs around the world. About half of the world's countries could be short of water by 2025, and almost 75% of the world's population by 205013. Climate change threatens our health due to increased disease, lack of clean water, smog and more14.

Melting ice caps and glaciers are destroying Arctic ecosystems. Melting ice is increasing sea levels and destroying the habitat of northern animals such as polar bears. Many polar bears are starving or drowning due to melting glaciers15. Ocean acidity has increased by 30% as the oceans absorb about 2 billion tons of carbon dioxide per year for the last 200 years16. Coral reefs in waters with a high concentration of carbon dioxide become less stable and solid, causing bleaching and eventual death of the coral17. By 2050, only 5% of the Great Barrier Reef will survive18. About half a billion people depend on coral reef fish as their main source of food19. Global warming causes hot, dry climates, which facilitate forest fires and destroy ecosystems and habitats for countless species20. Global warming will become the biggest cause of species extinctions this century. A temperature rise of 1.5°C can put 20 to 30% of species at risk of extinction21.
